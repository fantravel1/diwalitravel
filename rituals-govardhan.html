Govardhan Puja

Mountain Worship & Krishna Legend

Complete guide to Govardhan Puja, celebrated on the day after Diwali. Understand the Krishna legend, mountain and cow worship, Annakut festival, and how to participate in this sacred celebration.

Govardhan Puja ‚Äî Overview

Govardhan Puja is celebrated on the day after the main Diwali (Kartik Shukla Pratipada). It honors Lord Krishna and celebrates the triumph of devotion over arrogance. It's also known as Annakut, the festival of food offerings.

This is the second major ritual of Diwali week. If Lakshmi Puja celebrates prosperity, Govardhan Puja celebrates protection, devotion, and environmental respect (through cow and mountain worship).

The Krishna Legend ‚Äî Why We Celebrate

The Story of Govardhan

Setting: Mathura (a kingdom in North India). Krishna is a young cowherd.

The Conflict

  • King Indra (god of rain and storms) becomes angry with the village of Gokul
  • He sends torrential rains and a terrible storm
  • The villagers are terrified; their homes and livestock are threatened
  • They pray to Krishna for help

Krishna's Heroism

  • The solution: Krishna lifts Govardhan Mountain with one hand
  • The significance: Holds it above his head like an umbrella
  • The duration: Holds it for seven days and nights
  • The protection: All villagers and cattle shelter safely beneath

The Lesson

The story teaches several important lessons:

  • Devotion triumphs: Faith in Krishna protects more than any god's power
  • Environmental respect: Mountains and nature are sacred and worthy of worship
  • Humility over arrogance: Indra's arrogance is humbled by Krishna's simple act
  • Love transcends fear: The villagers' love for Krishna gives them courage

Govardhan Puja Rituals

Mountain Worship

  • Physical mountains: Devotees climb Govardhan Mountain (in Mathura) to worship
  • In homes: People create a small symbolic mountain from cow dung or clay
  • Decoration: Decorated with flowers, incense, and offerings
  • Circumambulation: Walking around the mountain seven times (like Krishna holding it 7 days)

Cow Worship (Gomata Puja)

Cows are sacred in Hinduism; Govardhan Puja especially honors them:

  • Cow decoration: Cows are bathed, decorated with flowers and garlands
  • Cow veneration: People bow to cows, touch their feet
  • Feeding cows: Special food (grass, grains, sweets) offered to cows
  • Why: Cows represent Mother Earth, nurture, and selfless giving

Annakut Festival (Food Offering)

"Anna" means food; "Kut" means mountain. It's a mountain of food offerings.

  • What it is: 56 or more varieties of food offered to Krishna
  • Significance: Celebrates gratitude for harvest and abundance
  • Foods included: Vegetables, grains, sweets, fruits, dairy products
  • After offering: Food is distributed as prasad to all devotees

Prayer & Chanting

  • Mantras invoking Krishna are chanted
  • "Govardhan Sthapan" - establishment of Govardhan
  • Stories of Krishna-Indra are recited
  • Devotional songs are sung

When is Govardhan Puja?

Date in 2026

  • Main Diwali: November 8, 2026
  • Govardhan Puja: November 9, 2026 (Monday)
  • Timing: Early morning, usually before sunrise

Calendar Placement

  • Part of five-day Diwali celebration
  • Day 3 of Diwali week (after Choti Diwali and Main Diwali)
  • Followed by Bhai Dooj (brother-sister celebration)

Govardhan Puja in Different Regions

Mathura & Vrindavan (Birthplace of Krishna)

Most elaborate celebrations:

  • Actual pilgrimage to Govardhan Mountain
  • Thousands of devotees circumambulate the mountain
  • Temples hold special ceremonies
  • Massive Annakut food festivals

North India (Most Common)

  • Govardhan mountain created at home from cow dung
  • Cow worship in homes and temples
  • Local variations in rituals

South India

  • Celebrated but less emphasis than North
  • Focus more on Krishna worship generally
  • Some temples celebrate with Annakut

Diaspora Communities

  • Temples organize Govardhan Puja
  • Cow worship adapted (some communities lack cows)
  • Annakut festivals with community participation

Participating in Govardhan Puja

Visiting Temples

  • Early morning arrival: Pujas often at 6-8 AM
  • Temple participation: Many temples hold special ceremonies
  • What to expect: Prayers, food offerings, distribution of prasad
  • Dress: Same as any temple - covered shoulders, long pants/skirts

Cow Worship

  • If you see cows being worshipped: Show respect
  • Can pet gently: Cows are often gentle and accustomed to devotees
  • Don't disturb: They're being fed and worshipped; respect the ritual
  • What it means: Honoring nature and gratitude for sustenance

Annakut Festival

  • Attending: Open to all; huge community gathering
  • Food sharing: Try the many varieties offered as prasad
  • Celebrating: Be part of the joy and gratitude

If Doing at Home

  • Create small Govardhan from clay or cow dung
  • Decorate with flowers and incense
  • Offer food and prayers to Krishna
  • Walk around it seven times (if possible)
  • Share food with family and neighbors

Understanding the Symbolism

Govardhan Mountain as Symbol

  • Mountain: Stability, steadiness, unchanging
  • Krishna holding it: Faith and devotion as protection
  • Umbrella effect: Spiritual shelter and safety
  • Seven days/circles: Cycles of time, completeness

Cow Symbolism

  • Nourishment: Cows provide milk (food)
  • Mother Earth: Cows embody connection to nature
  • Sacrifice: Cows ask nothing in return for giving
  • Gentleness: Kindness and non-aggression

Annakut Meaning

  • Gratitude: Thankfulness for harvest
  • Abundance: Celebration of plenty
  • Sharing: Food is meant to be shared
  • Community: Feast brings people together

Overall Lesson

Govardhan Puja teaches that:

  • Devotion and faith protect better than power
  • Nature (mountains, cows) deserves respect
  • Humility triumphs over arrogance
  • Abundance is meant to be shared

Common Questions

Is Govardhan less important than Lakshmi Puja?

Different, not less important. Lakshmi Puja focuses on prosperity; Govardhan Puja focuses on protection and devotion. Both are essential parts of Diwali.

What if I'm vegetarian? Are cows okay with that?

Yes! Vegetarianism aligns with cow veneration. Cows eat grass and plants; offering them vegetables and grains is perfect.

Can I participate if I'm not Hindu?

Absolutely. The message of protecting nature, respecting cows, and celebrating abundance is universal.

What is the actual Govardhan Mountain?

Real mountain in Mathura, birthplace of Krishna. Many pilgrims journey there to circumambulate it during Govardhan Puja.

How long is the circumambulation?

Full circuit of actual Govardhan Mountain: about 21 km (13 miles), taking 3-5 hours depending on pace and stops.

What's the difference between Govardhan Puja and Annakut?

Govardhan Puja is the primary ritual; Annakut is a specific aspect (food festival) that's part of the celebration.

Final Thoughts

Govardhan Puja carries lessons often forgotten in modern life: respect for nature, protection through faith, and humility before the sacred. The image of Krishna holding a mountain to protect people remains one of the most profound spiritual metaphors.

Whether you climb an actual mountain, create a symbolic one, worship cows, or simply enjoy the Annakut feast, you're participating in a 5,000-year-old tradition of gratitude and reverence.

May Krishna's protection guide you. May your devotion be steadfast as mountains.
